# Moats & Strategy

Use this when the user asks about durable competitive advantage, "what's my moat", strategic positioning, or industry analysis. Lead with **7 Powers** (Helmer) — it's the most useful framework for most modern businesses. Use Porter when the question is industry-structural. Use Thiel monopoly framing as the philosophical layer. Use Taleb for fat-tail/asymmetry framing.

---

## Hamilton Helmer's 7 Powers

A Power must produce **both a benefit and a barrier**. Most claimed moats are benefits without barriers.

### 1. Scale Economies
*Benefit:* Cost per unit declines with volume. *Barrier:* Smaller competitors can't match cost structure.
*Examples:* Costco (retail), AWS (infra), Netflix (content amortization), TSMC (manufacturing).
*Especially relevant for:* Hardware, infrastructure, capital-intensive businesses, marketplaces, content platforms.

### 2. Network Economies
*Benefit:* Each user makes the product more valuable to others. *Barrier:* New entrants face cold-start.
*Examples:* LinkedIn, Visa, Whatnot, Stripe (developer ecosystem).
*Especially relevant for:* Marketplaces, social products, developer platforms, payments networks.

### 3. Counter-Positioning
*Benefit:* You run a business model incumbents structurally can't copy without cannibalizing their core. *Barrier:* They'd rather lose to you than disrupt themselves.
*Examples:* Netflix DVDs vs. Blockbuster, Vanguard vs. active management, Robinhood vs. legacy brokers, Tesla vs. legacy auto.
*Especially relevant for:* Any disruption story. Industries with profitable incumbents whose model has structural weakness.

### 4. Switching Costs
*Benefit:* Customers face real cost (financial, procedural, relational, data) to leave. *Barrier:* Acquired customers stay even when better products appear.
*Examples:* Enterprise SaaS (Salesforce, ServiceNow), Apple/iCloud, your bank, Notion once your docs are inside.
*Especially relevant for:* Enterprise software, fintech, anywhere data accumulates over time.

### 5. Branding
*Benefit:* Customers pay a premium or extend trust based on brand. *Barrier:* Brand takes decades.
*Examples:* Hermès, Tiffany, Apple, Patagonia, Coca-Cola.
*Especially relevant for:* Consumer goods, hospitality, fashion, healthcare (clinician trust), regulated industries (where trust matters).

### 6. Cornered Resource
*Benefit:* Preferential access to a coveted asset (talent, IP, contract, raw material) on terms competitors can't match.
*Examples:* Pixar story brain trust (90s/00s), ASML on EUV lithography, specific drug patents, Tesla on Nevada lithium supply, OpenAI's compute commitments.
*Especially relevant for:* Biotech (IP), hardware (supply chain), AI (compute, data, talent), media (exclusive content).

### 7. Process Power
*Benefit:* Embedded organizational processes that produce superior output. *Barrier:* Long, hard-to-imitate development path.
*Examples:* Toyota Production System, SpaceX rapid iteration culture, Pixar story development, Stripe's API design discipline, Anthropic's safety research process.
*Especially relevant for:* Manufacturing, deep tech, AI labs, biotech (clinical operations), anywhere accumulated learning matters.

## Applying 7 Powers by stage and industry

### Early-stage startup (pre-PMF)
You almost certainly don't have any Power yet. That's normal. The question is: which Power could you plausibly earn over the next 2–5 years, and what compounds toward it?

### Common credible early Powers by industry:
- **Consumer mobile/social:** Branding (founder-led story, distinctive POV), eventually Network Effects (if social mechanics work), Switching Costs (data/progress).
- **Enterprise SaaS:** Switching Costs (deep integrations), eventually Scale Economies (R&D amortization).
- **Biotech:** Cornered Resource (IP), eventually Process Power (clinical/manufacturing learning).
- **Hardware:** Process Power (manufacturing learning curve), eventually Scale Economies.
- **Marketplace:** Network Economies is the canonical goal — but solving the cold-start (Andrew Chen) comes first.
- **Fintech:** Switching Costs (where money flows), regulatory licenses as Cornered Resource.
- **AI infrastructure:** Cornered Resource (talent, compute, data), eventually Process Power.
- **Creator economy / media:** Branding (the creator's IP), Switching Costs only if you accumulate something irreplaceable.

### Rare for indie/small companies:
- **Scale Economies** (requires meaningful fixed costs amortized over volume).
- **Counter-Positioning** (requires structural difference from incumbent, not just feature).
- **Cornered Resource at meaningful scale** (rare without capital).

## Thiel monopoly framing (philosophical layer)

1. **"Is this a monopoly business, or a competitive one?"** Thiel: competitive businesses make no money. Monopolies make all the money. Most founders mistake competitive businesses for monopolies.
2. **"What's the secret?"** What do you believe about this market that few others believe and that turns out to be true?
3. **"Why now?"** What changed in the world that makes this possible in 2026 that wasn't possible in 2020?
4. **"Definite or indefinite?"** Definite optimism (specific plan) > indefinite optimism ("things will work out").

## Porter's Five Forces (for industry-structural questions)

Use when entering a new market or analyzing why a market is/isn't profitable.

1. **Threat of new entrants** — How easy is it for new competitors to enter?
2. **Bargaining power of suppliers** — Are key inputs concentrated?
3. **Bargaining power of buyers** — Can buyers easily walk?
4. **Threat of substitutes** — What else solves the same job?
5. **Industry rivalry** — How intense is competition among existing players?

**By industry:**
- **iOS games / consumer mobile:** All five forces hostile. Niche-focus + brand is the only way out.
- **Enterprise SaaS:** Buyer power is high in commodity categories, low in entrenched workflows. Switching cost defenses are critical.
- **Biotech:** Supplier power (CDMOs, regulators) is structural. Substitutes (existing standard of care) are the real competition.
- **Hardware:** Supplier power often high (component concentration). Scale Economies are the response.
- **Climate hardware:** Regulatory environment is functionally the sixth force.

## Nassim Taleb — Antifragility and asymmetric exposure

Often missed in moat analysis but essential for capital-intensive, regulated, or long-cycle businesses.

### The barbell strategy
Don't allocate evenly across "medium-risk" bets. Allocate most resources to very safe positions (preservation) and a small portion to very high-upside asymmetric bets (optionality). Medium-risk is where most people lose money.

### Key questions to ask
- **What's your exposure to fat tails — positive *and* negative?** Black-swan downside risks are often invisible until they aren't.
- **Where do you have optionality?** Optionality = right but not obligation. Hedge your bets so you benefit from upside without being destroyed by downside.
- **Who has skin in the game?** Decision-makers who don't bear consequences make worse decisions.
- **Is this antifragile (gains from disorder) or fragile (breaks under stress)?**

### Applied by industry
- **Biotech:** Inherently fat-tailed (most drugs fail; the few that succeed return everything). Portfolio approach is the antifragile play.
- **Hardware:** Capital-intensive single bets can be fragile. Modular/platform approaches preserve optionality.
- **Consumer:** Many small bets > one big bet. Test cheaply before scaling.
- **Climate:** Long timelines amplify tail risk. Build for multiple scenarios.

## Generic strategies (Porter)

Pick **one**:
- **Cost leadership** — cheapest, win on price.
- **Differentiation** — uniquely valuable.
- **Focus** — serve one niche extraordinarily well.

Trying to be all three usually means being none. Indie / early-stage companies almost always win via **focus + differentiation**.

## Practical playbook: building a moat from zero

For any industry, when the user asks "how do I build a moat":

1. **Pick one Power to compound toward.** Don't try to build all seven. Match the choice to industry (see industry-by-industry list above).
2. **Identify what activity, every week, compounds toward that Power.** Brand → consistent voice. Process → documenting and improving the workflow. Switching costs → deepening value of accumulated data/integrations. Cornered resource → locking up the IP/contract/talent.
3. **Audit weekly: are you doing the compounding work, or just feature work?** Features don't compound. The Power-building activities do.

## Notes for the assistant

- **Don't over-claim moats.** Most early-stage businesses don't have one yet. Pretending they do is the failure mode.
- **The Powers test is benefit + barrier.** If only one half is present, it's not a Power yet.
- **Match the moat framework to the industry.** Consumer mobile ≠ biotech ≠ enterprise SaaS. The 7 Powers framework applies universally, but the *credible early Powers* differ enormously by domain.
- **Always pair moat analysis with Thiel monopoly framing.** "Even if you build that moat, is the underlying business actually a monopoly, or a slightly-better participant in a competitive market?"
