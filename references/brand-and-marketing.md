## Positioning — April Dunford

Positioning isn't a tagline. It's the deliberate act of placing your product *next to* a specific competitive alternative, on dimensions that matter to a specific customer.

### The 5 components

1. **Competitive alternatives** — What does the customer use today instead of you? (Not "our competitors" — *their* alternatives.)
2. **Unique attributes** — What does your product have that alternatives don't?
3. **Value (and proof)** — What does that unique attribute *enable* for the customer?
4. **Target customers** — Who values that enabled value most?
5. **Market category** — What context puts your product in the right mental frame?

### Running the exercise across industries

- **Consumer:** Alternatives may include "doing nothing" or "using a free alternative." Free competitors are often the real comparison.
- **Enterprise SaaS:** Alternatives often include "build it ourselves with spreadsheets" or "we already have a tool that mostly does this." Both are dangerous because they're invisible.
- **Biotech:** Alternatives are the existing standard of care. Differentiation must be clinically meaningful (efficacy, safety, dosing, route) — not just "novel mechanism."
- **Hardware:** Alternatives include both direct competitors and "the thing it replaces."
- **Fintech:** Alternatives often include cash, bank, or existing app. The status quo is a heavy competitor.
- **Marketplace:** Alternatives include "going direct" or "asking around." Don't compete only with other marketplaces.

## Strategic Narrative — Andy Raskin

For pitch decks, manifestos, About pages, founder essays. Five elements in order:

1. **Name the big shift.** Something is changing in the world.
2. **Show the stakes — winners and losers.**
3. **Tease the promised land.** What does the new world look like for winners?
4. **Identify the obstacles.**
5. **Present your "magic gift."** Your product overcomes the obstacles.

The story moves *from a changed world → to a problem → to your solution*. Most founder pitches do the opposite — solution first, problem second, world-shift buried.

## Growth — Andrew Chen + Brian Balfour

### Growth loops, not funnels

A funnel is linear: ad → install → activation → retention → revenue. A loop is recursive: a user does something → that produces an output → the output brings the next user.

**Loops by industry:**
- **Consumer:** Viral (invites, shares), content (UGC indexed by search), paid (LTV funds CAC).
- **Enterprise SaaS:** Product-led (Calendly meeting invite, Notion shared doc), content-led (SEO, podcasts), partner-led, sales-led with referral.
- **Marketplace:** Supply attracts demand attracts supply. Subsidize one side first.
- **Creator economy:** Audience growth → revenue → reinvestment in production quality → more audience.
- **Biotech:** Loops are different — it's clinical-data → KOL endorsement → broader adoption → more clinical data. Same recursive structure, longer cycle.

### Balfour's Four Fits

1. **Market ↔ Product Fit** (classic PMF).
2. **Product ↔ Channel Fit** — does your product fit the channels you'd grow through?
3. **Channel ↔ Model Fit** — does the channel work economically?
4. **Model ↔ Market Fit** — does monetization match what market will pay for and how?

Stuck growth = one of the four is broken. Diagnose which before prescribing.

## Brand-led Building — Weiss, Hewitt, Amoruso, Chouinard, Branson

Use when the question is "should I do more brand work" or "what does community-led growth actually look like."

### Common patterns across great brand-led companies
- **Audience-first.** Build community before product (Glossier started as a blog).
- **Distinctive aesthetic.** Visual identity is a brand asset.
- **Founder-as-channel** (Hewitt, Branson, Amoruso) — the founder's audience is the launch distribution.
- **Values-as-operating-system** (Chouinard) — what you say no to is as important as what you say yes to.
- **Consistency over time.** Brand-led businesses compound slowly. Every consistent touchpoint adds; every inconsistent one subtracts.

### When brand-led is the right move
- Consumer goods, hospitality, fashion, lifestyle, content.
- Industries where customers signal identity through purchase.
- Markets where functional differentiation is hard to sustain.

### When brand-led is the wrong move
- Pure utility purchases (most B2B infra, most healthcare workflows).
- Markets where customers prioritize price/spec over story.

## B2B GTM Cadence — David Sacks

Sacks's *Cadence* framework: structured operating rhythm prevents drift.

### Recommended cadences (paraphrased)
- **Weekly:** Sales team metrics review, top-deal review.
- **Monthly:** Marketing-funnel review, pipeline review, cohort retention review.
- **Quarterly:** Strategy review, OKR planning, customer-success review.
- **Annually:** Annual planning, comp review, board strategic offsite.

### Choosing the right motion
- **PLG (Product-Led Growth)** — best for low ACV ($0–$1K/month), self-serve buyer, single-user-can-decide products.
- **PLG + Sales-Assisted** — best for mid-ACV ($1K–$10K/month), multi-user, but no procurement.
- **Sales-Led** — best for high ACV ($10K+/month), multi-stakeholder, procurement.

**Common failure:** Forcing sales-led motion onto a product with PLG mechanics, or vice versa.

## Distribution — Nikita Bier (consumer apps)

(Already in `industry-playbooks.md` — quick reference here.)

- **The screenshot is the product.** App Store, friend's phone, share asset.
- **First 60 seconds.** Install to first delight in <60s.
- **Pull vs. push.** App Store search, social shares, word of mouth (pull) > ads (push) for indie.
- **Social object.** What spreads outside the product?
- **App Store positioning is a moat.** Owning a search term is durable distribution.

## Offer construction — Alex Hormozi

When conversion is the problem, not awareness. **Value Equation:**

> **Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)**

Improve perceived value by:
- Making the **dream outcome** more vivid.
- Increasing **perceived likelihood** (proof, guarantees, testimonials).
- Reducing **time delay** (faster results).
- Reducing **effort & sacrifice** (less friction).

When a product isn't converting, work the four levers — find the weakest.

## Sutherland's counterintuitive moves

Behavioral economics often beats logic. Some Sutherland principles worth keeping in mind:

- **"The opposite of a good idea can also be a good idea."** Don't assume one direction is right.
- **Psychological value > functional value** in many consumer purchases.
- **Reduce friction in one moment that matters** rather than improving averages.
- **Pricing signals quality** more than people admit. Cheaper isn't always better positioned.
